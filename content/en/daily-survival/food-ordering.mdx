The Chinese restaurant experience for non-speakers has become easier with technology — the very thing that makes payment intimidating also removes the need to talk to a waiter. Here are your options, ranked from easiest to most challenging.

## Menu Decoder: What You're Looking At

Chinese menus organize dishes into categories that repeat across nearly every restaurant. Recognizing these 6 category headers cuts your translation time by half. Look for these characters on a paper menu or in a scan-to-order app:

| Chinese | Pinyin | English | What to Expect |
|---|---|---|---|
| 主食 | zhǔshí | Staples / Mains | Rice (米饭), fried rice (炒饭), noodles (面/粉), dumplings (饺子) |
| 热菜 | rècài | Hot Dishes | Stir-fried meat and vegetables, braised dishes, stews — the core of a Chinese meal |
| 凉菜 | liángcài | Cold Dishes | Pickled vegetables, smashed cucumber (拍黄瓜), cold sliced beef, tofu skin |
| 汤 | tāng | Soup | Egg drop soup (蛋花汤), hot and sour soup (酸辣汤), daily soup (例汤) |
| 饮品 | yǐnpǐn | Drinks | Bottled water (矿泉水), tea (茶), soda (可乐/雪碧), fresh juice (鲜榨果汁) |
| 小吃 | xiǎochī | Snacks / Sides | Spring rolls, scallion pancakes, edamame, fried mantou (馒头) |

In scan-to-order apps, these categories appear as tabs or section headers. The app usually shows a small thumbnail photo next to each dish name — tap the photo to enlarge it before adding.

## Method 1: Scan-to-Order QR Code (Easiest)

The majority of sit-down restaurants in China — from noodle shops to hot pot chains — use a scan-to-order system. Here is how it works:

1. **Sit down at any available table.** Hosts are uncommon in casual restaurants. Choose an empty, clean table and sit.
2. **Find the QR code sticker on the table.** It is usually a square sticker with a QR code, sometimes labeled "扫码点餐" (scan to order). Look for it taped to the table edge, propped up in a plastic stand, or printed on the napkin holder.
3. **Open WeChat or Alipay and scan the code.** The scan function is built into both apps. Point your camera at the code, and the app opens a web-based menu.
4. **Use a translation app.** The menu is in Chinese. Take a screenshot, then open it in Google Translate or Microsoft Translator's camera/image mode. The translation is not perfect, but "chicken," "beef," "rice," and "noodles" are reliably translated. Look for:
   - Dish names — scan the characters left-to-right
   - Prices — clearly marked next to each item, typically ¥15-¥68 for a single dish in casual restaurants
   - Spice indicators — 辣椒 (chili pepper), 🌶 symbols (1-3 chilies = spice level), 麻辣 (numbing-and-spicy, the Sichuan signature)
5. **Tap to add dishes to your order.**
6. **Review your order.** The system shows a total in RMB.
7. **Submit the order.** The kitchen receives it immediately. Payment happens at the end (also through the same QR code system — tap "pay" and complete the transaction through Alipay or WeChat Pay), or you pay at the counter after eating.

**No human interaction required.** The most language-dependent act in daily life has been almost entirely automated.

### What the Scan-to-Order Screen Looks Like

After scanning, you see a mobile web page with:
- **Top banner:** Restaurant name in large Chinese characters, sometimes with a logo
- **Category tabs:** 推荐 (Recommended), 主食 (Staples), 热菜 (Hot Dishes), 凉菜 (Cold Dishes), 饮品 (Drinks)
- **Dish cards:** Each dish gets a thumbnail photo on the left, dish name and price on the right, and a "+" button to add
- **Bottom bar:** Shopping cart icon with running total, and a "下单" (Place Order) button

The interface works like any food delivery app. Scroll vertically through categories, tap "+" on dishes you want, and the cart updates in real time.

## Method 2: Picture Menus and Pointing

If there is no QR code, or the system requires a Chinese phone number (rare but happens):

1. **Look for a menu with pictures.** Most restaurants catering to any level of foot traffic have picture menus. If the menu handed to you has no pictures, say "有图片的菜单吗?" (yǒu túpiàn de càidān ma — "Do you have a menu with pictures?") or simply point to a menu at a neighboring table and gesture.
2. **Point at the pictures of what you want.** Hold up fingers for quantity. Say "这个" (zhège — "this one") while pointing. It works every time.
3. **Check the price.** Numbers are universal. If a dish says ¥38, that is 38 RMB.
4. **Write down or translate dietary restrictions in advance.** If you have allergies or dietary requirements, have these written in Chinese on your phone before entering the restaurant:
   - "我对花生过敏" — I am allergic to peanuts
   - "我不吃肉" — I do not eat meat (for vegetarians)
   - "我不吃辣" — I do not eat spicy food
   - "不要味精" — No MSG
5. **Use a translation app in conversation mode** for anything beyond simple pointing. Speak into the app, show the waiter the translation.

### Essential Pointing Phrases

You can order an entire meal by mastering two words and one sentence:

- **"这个"** (zhège) — "This one." Point at the picture, say this, and the waiter writes it down. Use it for every dish.
- **"我要这个"** (wǒ yào zhège) — "I want this one." Slightly more polite than just "这个." Use it as your default ordering phrase.
- **"不要放辣椒"** (bùyào fàng làjiāo) — "Don't add chili." Use this when ordering ANY dish in Sichuan, Hunan, Guizhou, or Jiangxi provinces, even if the dish doesn't look spicy. These regional cuisines default to spicy. "不辣" (bù là — not spicy) is too vague — chefs hear it as "mild" rather than "none." Say "不要放辣椒" for zero chili.

### The "请不要放辣椒" Distinction

"请不要放辣椒" (qǐng bùyào fàng làjiāo) means "Please do not add chili peppers." It is stronger and more polite than "不辣" (bù là). Here is when to use each:

| Phrase | Meaning | When to Use |
|---|---|---|
| 不辣 (bù là) | Not spicy | At non-Sichuan/Hunan restaurants where you want mild food |
| 不要辣椒 (bùyào làjiāo) | No chili | At any restaurant where you want zero heat — moderately firm |
| 请不要放辣椒 (qǐng bùyào fàng làjiāo) | Please don't add chili | At Sichuan, Hunan, or Guizhou restaurants where the default is spicy — the most effective version |
| 我一点辣都不能吃 (wǒ yīdiǎn là dōu bùnéng chī) | I can't eat any spice at all | Emergency phrase — use when the first request was ignored |

In practice, saying "请不要放辣椒" when you order and repeating it if the waiter seems unsure covers 95% of situations. Write it on your phone and show it if you need to.

## Method 3: Food Delivery Apps

**Meituan** (美团) and **Ele.me** (饿了么) are the two dominant food delivery platforms. Together they process over 60 million orders per day nationwide. Both are Chinese-language apps, but with a translation app and patience, they are usable.

### Meituan App Walkthrough

![Chinese food ordering guide](/images/articles/food-ordering.png)

1. **Download Meituan** from your app store. The icon is a yellow kangaroo on a yellow background. Search "美团" or "Meituan."
2. **Register with your phone number.** Meituan now accepts +1, +44, +61, and other international codes. You receive an SMS verification code. If SMS fails (happens to about 10% of foreign numbers), try the voice call verification option.
3. **Set your delivery address.** Tap the address bar at the top. Paste your hotel address in Chinese characters. The app auto-detects nearby restaurants — typically 200-500 options within a 3 km radius in major cities.
4. **Browse restaurants.** The home screen shows:
   - Search bar at the top
   - Category row: 美食 (Food), 甜点饮品 (Desserts & Drinks), 超市便利 (Supermarket), 水果 (Fruit)
   - Restaurant cards below: each shows a photo, star rating (4.0-5.0), monthly order count, delivery time (typically 25-45 minutes), and delivery fee (usually ¥0-¥5)
5. **Enter a restaurant.** Tap any restaurant card. The menu page shows dish photos, names, prices, and monthly order counts for popular items. Tap a dish to see a full-screen photo.
6. **Add to cart.** Tap the orange "+" button next to any dish. Tap it again to increase quantity. The app shows allergens and spice levels for some chains, but most independent restaurants do not — use your translation app.
7. **Check out.** Tap the shopping cart icon, review your order, tap "去结算" (Go to Checkout).
8. **Confirm and pay.** Verify the address and total, then tap "提交订单" (Submit Order). Choose Alipay or WeChat Pay and complete the payment.

### Ele.me Alternative

Ele.me (blue icon with an "e") follows the same pattern. Its interface is slightly cleaner than Meituan. Registration and payment work identically. Ele.me tends to have more international chain restaurants in major cities; Meituan has broader coverage of local restaurants.

### When the Rider Calls

The delivery rider will call when they arrive. This is the one unavoidable verbal interaction. Riders speak rapid, accented Chinese and typically say only three things:
- "外卖到了" (wàimài dào le) — "Your delivery has arrived"
- "你在哪里" (nǐ zài nǎlǐ) — "Where are you?"
- "放哪里" (fàng nǎlǐ) — "Where should I put it?"

Prepare for this. Have your hotel front desk phone number ready. Better yet, use the in-app chat — Meituan and Ele.me both have a text chat with the rider. Send them "请放前台" (qǐng fàng qiántái — please leave at the front desk) as soon as they pick up your order. Many riders will comply without calling.

### Delivery Tip

Both Meituan and Ele.me charge a small delivery fee (¥0-¥8 depending on distance and restaurant). Tipping is not expected and there is no in-app tip field. Delivery time estimates are generally accurate within 5 minutes.

## Finding English-Friendly Restaurants with Dianping

**Dianping** (大众点评) is China's Yelp — a restaurant discovery and review platform with 300 million monthly active users. Its mobile app is Chinese-only, but you can use the mini-program inside WeChat or Alipay without installing a separate app.

**How to use Dianping as a foreigner:**

1. Open WeChat or Alipay, search for "大众点评" in the mini-program search bar.
2. Allow location access. The app shows restaurants near you, ranked by rating and popularity.
3. Look for these indicators of foreigner-friendliness:
   - Photos of English menus in the restaurant's photo gallery
   - Reviews mentioning "外国人" (foreigner), "英文菜单" (English menu), or "老外" (laowai — colloquial term for foreigner)
   - High photo count of food — picture-heavy restaurants are easier to order at
   - "必吃榜" (Must-Eat List) badge — these are Dianping's curated top picks, and they typically have higher service standards
4. Tap the "地址" (address) section to get navigation directions through your map app.

**Search filters you can use without reading Chinese:**
- Sort by "好评优先" (Best Reviews First) — always your default
- Filter by "距离最近" (Nearest) — sorts by proximity
- Category filters: 火锅 (Hot Pot), 烧烤 (BBQ), 日料 (Japanese), 西餐 (Western), 咖啡厅 (Cafe)

Copy a restaurant's Chinese name and address into your notes before heading out. Show it to a taxi driver or paste it into your ride-hailing app.

## Method 4: Chain Restaurants with English Menus

Some restaurant types are reliably English-friendly:

- **Western chains:** McDonald's, KFC, Burger King, Pizza Hut, Starbucks — all have English menu options at self-service kiosks. McDonald's and KFC kiosks support English with a single tap on the language switcher (top-right corner). These kiosks also accept foreign credit cards directly — no Alipay or WeChat required.
- **Japanese chains:** Yoshinoya (吉野家), Ajisen Ramen (味千拉面) — picture menus are standard, and the food is consistent. Self-service kiosks have English mode at larger locations.
- **Hot pot chains:** Haidilao (海底捞), Xiabu Xiabu (呷哺呷哺) — iPad ordering with pictures and some English labels. Haidilao in major cities (Beijing, Shanghai, Shenzhen, Guangzhou, Chengdu) provides English-speaking staff on request. Ask "有会说英文的服务员吗?" (yǒu huì shuō yīngwén de fúwùyuán ma).
- **Hotel restaurants:** Most international hotel restaurants have English menus. Marriott, Hilton, InterContinental, and Hyatt properties in China all provide bilingual menus. These are more expensive (¥80-¥200 per dish) but eliminate language friction entirely.

## Method 5: Convenience Store Meals

When all else fails, convenience stores (FamilyMart, 7-Eleven, Lawson, and local chains like Meiyijia/美宜佳) sell ready-to-eat meals, sandwiches, onigiri, and instant noodles. There are approximately 350,000 convenience stores across China, with 3-5 within a 10-minute walk in any urban area.

Staff will heat food for you — just point and smile. At the counter, hand them your selected items and say "加热" (jiārè — heat up). They scan, heat, and hand back your food in under 2 minutes.

**What to get at a Chinese convenience store:**
- **Onigiri / rice balls** (饭团): ¥3-¥8, labeled with fillings like tuna (金枪鱼), pickled plum (梅子), or chicken (鸡肉)
- **Bento boxes** (盒饭/便当): ¥12-¥25, a full meal with rice, meat, and vegetables
- **Steamed buns** (包子): ¥2-¥5 each, in a steamer cabinet near the counter — pork (猪肉), vegetable (素菜), red bean (豆沙)
- **Instant noodles** (方便面): ¥4-¥10, with hot water dispenser available in-store
- **Sandwiches** (三明治): ¥6-¥15, egg salad, ham, or chicken

These stores operate 24 hours in urban areas. If you arrive on a late flight, a FamilyMart bento and a cold beer cost you about ¥20-¥30 total.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Tapping "confirm" on a dish you do not recognize.** If you are unsure, screenshot and translate the dish name before adding it to your order. Scan-to-order systems commit your order immediately.
- **Spice level assumptions.** "Not spicy" in parts of Sichuan and Hunan still means "somewhat spicy" by international standards. If you genuinely cannot handle any heat, use "请不要放辣椒" and be prepared to repeat it. In Chengdu and Changsha, expect pushback — the local food culture treats spice as non-negotiable.
- **Serving style.** Chinese meals are typically shared. Dishes are placed in the center of the table and everyone takes portions. Individual plates and "one dish per person" ordering is not the norm. Order 2-3 dishes for 2 people, 3-4 for 3 people, and so on. Rice (米饭) is ordered separately — it does not come with dishes automatically. It costs ¥2-¥5 per bowl.
- **Water.** Tap water is not served in Chinese restaurants and is not safe to drink anywhere in China. Bottled water (矿泉水, ¥3-¥5), tea (茶 — often free or ¥2-¥5 per person), and soft drinks (可乐/雪碧, ¥5-¥8) are the options. If you want tea, it is typically refilled throughout the meal at no extra charge.
- **Payment timing.** In casual restaurants, you pay after eating — either through the QR code system or at the counter. In very small, local eateries, you may need to pay at the counter before leaving. When in doubt, watch what other customers do after finishing their meal.
- **Napkins and utensils.** Napkins (餐巾纸) are often not provided or come in a small tissue packet that costs ¥1-¥2. Carry your own tissues. Chopsticks and spoons are on the table or in a drawer built into the table — reach down and pull it open.
- **Tipping.** Zero tipping culture. Do not leave cash on the table. It will either be ignored or chased after you by a confused waiter. Service charges (10-15%) only appear at high-end hotel restaurants and are clearly marked on the bill.

## Step-by-Step Guide (Scan-to-Order Method)

1. Enter the restaurant and find an empty table.
2. Locate the QR code on the table.
3. Scan with WeChat or Alipay.
4. Screenshot the menu page.
5. Open the screenshot in a translation app.
6. Tap dishes that look appealing and fit your dietary needs.
7. Check the total and submit.
8. Wait for the food to arrive (typically 10-20 minutes).
9. Eat. If you need anything (more napkins, water, the bill), flag down a staff member and use your translation app.
10. When finished, tap "结账" (Pay) in the QR code system, or go to the counter and show your table number. If paying at the counter, say "买单" (mǎidān — "the bill please") or simply hold up your phone with the payment QR code ready.
