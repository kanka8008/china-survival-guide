Departing a Chinese airport follows a fixed sequence. The process is straightforward but has more steps than most Western airports. Knowing the steps in order prevents backtracking.

## How Early to Arrive

**3 hours before departure** is the recommended minimum for international flights. Major airports (Beijing Capital, Shanghai Pudong, Guangzhou Baiyun) are large and checkpoints are spread out. During peak hours (early mornings, Friday evenings, Chinese holidays), add an extra 30-45 minutes.

If you are claiming a tax refund, opening hours for the tax refund counter may be limited — confirm in advance and factor in queue time.

## Entering the Departure Hall

The international departure hall at major Chinese airports is a vast open space with high ceilings, digital flight information screens, and rows of airline check-in counters labeled by letter (A through M, for example). Walking in, you see:

- **Flight Information Boards** — massive LED screens showing flight numbers, destinations, check-in counter assignments, and boarding status. Find your flight number and note the check-in counter letter.
- **Security Check at the Entrance** — Before reaching the check-in area, a security officer may check your passport and flight booking at the hall entrance. Have them ready.
- **Customs Declaration Desk** — if you are carrying items that require declaration (large amounts of cash, high-value goods), you complete a declaration form here, before check-in.
- **Tax Refund Counter** — usually near the international departures entrance, before the security checkpoint. Look for signs saying "离境退税" or "Tax Refund."

## Step-by-Step Guide

### Step 1: Enter the Correct Terminal

Chinese airports often have multiple terminals separated by significant distances. Check your booking confirmation for the terminal number. Beijing Capital Airport has three terminals (T1, T2, T3) connected by shuttle, not walking distance. Going to the wrong terminal adds 20-30 minutes.

### Step 2: Check In and Drop Luggage

Find your airline's check-in counter on the departure board. At the counter:
- Present your passport
- The agent verifies your booking and checks your entry/visa status
- Check your luggage (verify the baggage tag shows your final destination)
- Receive your boarding pass

**If claiming a tax refund with items in checked luggage:** Tell the check-in agent: "I have tax refund items in this bag." They will tag your bag but **not send it through the belt**. Take the tagged bag with you to the customs tax refund counter (Step 3), then return to the check-in counter or the oversized baggage drop to send the bag through.

### Step 3: The Departure Card

You do not fill out a separate departure card at Chinese airports as of recent policy changes. The exit immigration process is fully electronic — the officer scans your passport and all information is retrieved from the entry system. However, **always check the screens or signage** at the immigration area. If a departure card is required for your nationality, a stack of blank cards and a pen are available at a small desk before the immigration queue. Fill it out before you join the line.

### Step 4: Customs Tax Refund (If Applicable)

If you are claiming a departure tax refund on purchases:
1. Before security, go to the customs tax refund counter (near the international departures entrance). Look for the green sign with "离境退税" in white text.
2. Present your Tax Refund Application Form, sales invoices, passport, and boarding pass.
3. The officer may ask to inspect the purchased items — have them accessible.
4. The officer stamps your form.
5. If items were in your checked luggage (handled in Step 2), the stamped form goes through, and you return the luggage to the check-in counter.

### Step 5: Exit Immigration (Border Control)

After check-in and tax refund, proceed to **exit immigration** (出境 / chūjìng). Look for "International Departures" signs.

The immigration area at major airports is a hall of numbered counters. Queue in the **Foreigners** lane — look for "外国人" or a sign with a globe icon. Chinese citizens use a separate set of counters.

At the immigration counter:
1. Hand your passport and boarding pass to the officer.
2. The officer scans your passport. The system checks:
   - That you have not overstayed your permitted duration
   - That your entry and exit records match
   - Any flags or alerts in the system
3. You may be asked basic questions: "When did you arrive?" "Where did you stay?" Answer directly and briefly.
4. If you are using an e-gate, follow the on-screen instructions: scan your passport, place your finger on the fingerprint reader, look at the camera for facial recognition. The gate opens when verification is complete.
5. The officer stamps your passport with an **exit stamp** (出境章). The stamp is a small oval ink stamp showing the date and port of departure.
6. If you used an e-gate, no physical exit stamp appears — the exit record is electronic.

**What they stamp:** Officers stamp your passport, not your boarding pass. The exit stamp is separate from your entry stamp. Check the stamp is legible before you walk away. A smudged stamp causes problems at your next destination.

**Important:** If you have a visa that has not expired, the officer may ask about it. If you have an overstay, this is where it is detected — see the overstay penalty section for what to expect.

### Step 6: Security Screening

After immigration, proceed to security screening. The security area has multiple lanes with conveyor belts and metal detectors.

**What you remove from your bag and place in a tray:**
- Laptop computers (must be out of their sleeve/case)
- Tablets (iPad, etc.)
- Power banks (充电宝 / chōngdiànbǎo) — **must be in carry-on, never in checked luggage**
- Liquids in containers up to 100ml each, all fitting in a single 1-liter clear resealable bag
- Cameras and large electronic devices
- Metal objects, belts, watches, keys
- Jackets and coats

**Strict rules at Chinese airport security:**
- **Liquids over 100ml per container** are confiscated, even if the bottle is only partially full. A 200ml bottle with 50ml of liquid inside is confiscated. Transfer small amounts of liquids to 100ml travel bottles.
- **Power banks without a clearly printed capacity label (in mAh)** are confiscated. Check your power bank. If the label has worn off, buy a new one before the airport.
- **Power banks over 27,000 mAh (100 Wh)** are prohibited — both carry-on and checked. The capacity limit is printed on the power bank. Above 100 Wh, you need airline approval. Above 160 Wh, banned entirely.
- **Lighters and matches** are confiscated. One small personal lighter is sometimes permitted in your pocket (not in your bag), but do not count on it.

Walk through the metal detector when the security officer signals. They may perform an additional pat-down or swab test on your hands or bags.

### Step 7: Tax Refund Agent (Collect Your Refund)

After security, find the **Tax Refund Agent** counter in the departure lounge. Present:
- Your customs-stamped Tax Refund Application Form
- Passport
- Boarding pass

Choose your refund method: cash (RMB), bank card refund, or Alipay. Cash is immediate. Bank card and Alipay take days to process.

### Step 8: Proceed to Your Gate

Check the departure board for your gate number. Gates at major Chinese airports can be a 10-15 minute walk from the central departure area. Boarding typically begins 30-40 minutes before departure and closes 10-15 minutes before departure.

## The Tax Refund Counter — What It Looks Like

The customs tax refund counter (before security) is a small desk or booth, usually with:
- A green sign reading "离境退税 Customs Tax Refund"
- A countertop with a stamp pad and scanner
- One or two officers in customs uniform
- A small queue area marked with floor stickers

The Refund Agent counter (after security) is a bank-like window:
- Marked with bank logos (Bank of China typically operates these)
- A small digital screen showing refund rates
- A queue line with a rope barrier
- A cash tray for RMB disbursement

## Common Pitfalls

- **Arriving only 2 hours early.** Major Chinese international airports handle large volumes of travelers. Queues at check-in, immigration, and security can each take 20-40 minutes during peak times. 2 hours is tight; 3 hours is comfortable.
- **Not checking terminal numbers.** Beijing Capital T3 arrivals and T2 departures are 15 minutes apart by shuttle bus. Taxi drivers will take you to the terminal you tell them — confirm the right one before leaving your hotel.
- **Power banks in checked luggage.** This is consistently flagged. Power banks and spare lithium batteries must be in carry-on luggage. If discovered in checked luggage, your bag may be opened and the items removed without you present.
- **Forgetting the exit stamp check.** Before leaving the immigration counter, glance at the exit stamp to confirm it is legible. A smudged or missing exit stamp can cause issues at your next destination or when re-entering China in the future.
- **Leaving tax refund items in checked luggage without declaring them first.** If customs wants to inspect the items and your bag is already loaded on the plane, the refund is denied. Follow the procedure in Steps 2-4 carefully.
- **Liquids purchased after security.** Items purchased in the departure lounge (duty-free liquids, bottled water) are permitted on the aircraft, but if your flight connects through another airport with additional security screening (e.g., transiting through a different country), those liquids may be confiscated at the transit point.
