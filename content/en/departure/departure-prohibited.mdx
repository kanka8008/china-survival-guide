### What items are completely banned from leaving China?

The departure prohibited list largely mirrors the entry prohibited list, plus several categories specific to cultural heritage and national security:

1. **All items on the entry prohibited list** — weapons, ammunition, counterfeit currency, narcotics, toxic substances, and items deemed harmful to state interests
2. **Classified documents and materials** — anything containing state secrets or sensitive information
3. **Protected cultural relics** — antiques and artifacts that require an export permit
4. **Endangered wildlife and products derived from them** — ivory, rhino horn, tiger bone, certain traditional medicines, coral, and products made from protected species
5. **Endangered plants and seeds** — certain plant species, seeds, and derivatives that are protected under CITES (Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species)
6. **Valuable cultural items without authorization** — rare books, manuscripts, artworks, and historical documents that the Cultural Heritage Administration has designated as protected

### What the Prohibited Items Poster Shows

At every Chinese airport, before the security checkpoint, you see a large poster or digital screen listing prohibited items. The poster typically shows:
- Photographs of prohibited items with red X marks
- Quantities and thresholds for restricted items
- Bilingual text (Chinese and English)
- A notice about criminal penalties for wildlife products

**Standard restricted quantities shown on the poster:**

| Item | Limit | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Liquor (白酒 / 酒精饮料)** | **2 bottles** (up to 1.5 liters total) | Covers all alcoholic beverages. Chinese baijiu, wine, beer — all count toward the same 2-bottle/1.5L limit. |
| **Cigarettes** | **400 cigarettes** (2 cartons) | OR 100 cigars, OR 500 grams of tobacco. You cannot mix these quotas. |
| **RMB Cash** | **20,000 yuan** | Physical banknotes. No declaration needed below this amount. |
| **Foreign Currency** | **USD 5,000 equivalent** | Without declaration. USD 5,000-10,000 requires a declaration form and bank certificate. |
| **Gold/Silver** | **50 grams** | Above this requires a People's Bank of China permit. |
| **Tea** | Reasonable personal use | Bulk commercial quantities trigger export requirements. |
| **Silk** | Reasonable personal use | Same as tea — personal quantities are fine; bulk commercial quantities require documentation. |

### What are the cash limits for leaving China?

The same rules apply on departure as on entry:
- **RMB:** Maximum 20,000 yuan in physical cash
- **Foreign currency:** Up to USD 5,000 equivalent without declaration; USD 5,000-10,000 with declaration and a bank certificate; above USD 10,000 requires a SAFE permit

If you brought cash into China and declared it at entry (with a stamped declaration form), show that form at departure to account for the cash you are taking out. Without it, the cash is treated as undeclared on exit.

### Can I take antiques and artwork out of China?

**It depends on the age and classification of the item.**

- **Items manufactured before 1949** (the founding of the People's Republic of China) may be classified as cultural relics. Taking them out of China requires an **Export Permit for Cultural Relics** issued by the National Cultural Heritage Administration or its authorized local offices.
- **Items manufactured in or after 1949** that are mass-produced and have no particular historical significance can generally be exported without a permit.
- **Original paintings, calligraphy, and sculptures by recognized artists** may require an export permit regardless of age, especially if the artist is culturally significant.
- **Items purchased from a licensed antique shop** with a red official export seal already applied can be exported — this is the safest way to buy antiques as a foreigner.

### The Cultural Relics Export Certificate

When you buy an antique that qualifies for export from a licensed dealer, the shop affixes a **red wax seal** (火漆印) directly to the item. This seal is a small, round, red stamp of hardened wax that states the item passed cultural heritage review. You cannot remove it without damaging the seal. At the airport, customs officers look for this seal. An antique without the seal is subject to inspection and possible confiscation.

**To get an export certificate independently (not through a dealer):**
1. Take the item to the local **Cultural Heritage Administration** (文物局) for evaluation.
2. Submit an application form, your passport, and proof of purchase.
3. Pay an appraisal fee (varies by item value).
4. If approved, you receive an **Export Permit for Cultural Relics** — a printed certificate with the item description, your name, and an official stamp.
5. Processing takes 1-2 weeks. Do this well before your departure.

If you buy an antique item and are unsure about its export status, check with the seller. Licensed antique dealers know which items require permits. Unlicensed street vendors may sell items that cannot legally leave the country — the risk of confiscation at the airport is yours, not the seller's.

### What Happens When Customs Finds a Prohibited Item

When a customs officer flags your bag during screening, this is what happens:

1. The officer pulls your bag aside and asks you to open it.
2. They identify the prohibited item and explain the violation.
3. Depending on the item:
   - **Minor restricted item (slightly over the cigarette/liquor limit):** The excess is confiscated. You receive a confiscation notice. No further penalty.
   - **Antique without export permit:** The item is confiscated. You may be questioned about where you bought it. No fine for a first-time unintentional violation, but the item is gone.
   - **Wildlife product (ivory, rhino horn, etc.):** The item is confiscated immediately. You are detained for questioning. Police may be called. This is a criminal offense. Expect a formal investigation, possible charges, and severe penalties.
   - **Large undeclared cash:** The excess cash is seized. You must provide proof of the cash's origin. Undeclared cash above USD 10,000 equivalent without a permit triggers a formal customs investigation.
   - **Sensitive materials (politically sensitive documents, data):** Device or documents seized. You are detained for further investigation. The outcome depends on the nature of the material.

4. In all cases, you receive an official customs notice documenting the seizure. You sign it. This notice is filed in the customs system.

5. **You will miss your flight** if the inspection takes significant time. Customs does not delay inspections so you can catch your departure. The process takes priority.

### What about wildlife products?

Chinese customs enforcement on wildlife products has become significantly stricter in recent years, aligned with international CITES regulations. The following are high-risk:

- **Ivory and ivory products** — a near-total ban is in effect. Even old ivory items purchased abroad and carried through China may be seized.
- **Rhino horn** — absolutely prohibited.
- **Tiger bone, leopard bone, bear bile products** — prohibited.
- **Certain traditional Chinese medicines** — products containing musk, saiga antelope horn, pangolin scales, and other protected species derivatives are banned from international trade.
- **Coral, certain shells, and marine products** from protected species.
- **Live animals and plants** — generally require CITES permits and quarantine certificates.

If you are transiting through China with wildlife products purchased in a third country, these rules still apply. The item is in your possession within Chinese territory, and Chinese law applies.

### What about tea, silk, and common souvenirs?

Common consumer goods purchased for personal use — tea, silk scarves, porcelain, chopsticks, clothing, electronics, cosmetics — can be taken out without restriction as long as quantities are reasonable for personal use and the items are not on the prohibited list.

Bulk quantities of the same item (e.g., 50 identical silk scarves) may be treated as commercial export, which has different customs requirements.

### What are the penalties for taking prohibited items out?

- **Items confiscated** at the security or customs checkpoint — you will not receive compensation
- **Fines** — up to several times the value of the items
- **Criminal charges** — for endangered wildlife products, serious cases result in criminal prosecution
- **Missed flight** — inspections take time, and you may be held until the situation is resolved
- **Record of violation** — flagged in the customs system, affecting future entry and exit

### How to check if an item is safe to take out

1. **Ask the seller** at the time of purchase — licensed retailers know the export rules for their products
2. **Check the item for an export seal** — legitimate antique shops apply a red wax seal (火漆印) indicating the item has been cleared for export
3. **When in doubt, do not buy it** — the financial loss of confiscation and the stress of being stopped at customs outweigh the value of a questionable souvenir
4. **Research CITES listings** for any wildlife-derived product before purchase

### What about laptops, hard drives, and data?

Customs officers have the authority to inspect electronic devices and their contents at the border. While this is not routine for every traveler, it can happen. Data that is classified, politically sensitive, or violates Chinese law can result in the device being held or the traveler being detained for questioning.

This is a rare occurrence for ordinary tourists, but travelers should be aware that border search powers in China include digital device inspection.

### Quick Pre-Departure Luggage Checklist

Before leaving your hotel, open your bags and check:

- [ ] No ivory, rhino horn, or wildlife products of any kind
- [ ] No antiques without a red wax export seal
- [ ] No more than 20,000 RMB in physical cash
- [ ] No more than USD 5,000 equivalent in foreign currency (without declaration)
- [ ] No more than 2 bottles / 1.5L of liquor
- [ ] No more than 400 cigarettes / 2 cartons
- [ ] No classified documents or sensitive materials
- [ ] All power banks in carry-on, not checked luggage
- [ ] All liquids in containers of 100ml or less
- [ ] Laptop, tablet, and electronics accessible for security screening
