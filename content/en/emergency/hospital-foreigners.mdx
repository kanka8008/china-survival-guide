### Can foreigners go to any hospital in China?

**Yes, technically.** Any public hospital in China must treat any person in need of emergency care. There is no requirement to be a Chinese citizen or resident.

**In practice, the experience varies dramatically.** Public hospitals in China are high-volume institutions. Registration, payment, doctor consultations, tests, and pharmacy visits happen in different locations within a large complex, and nearly all signage and communication is in Chinese. The system is designed for efficiency at scale, not for guiding a non-Chinese speaker through the process.

For non-emergency care, foreigners should use either:
- **International hospitals** — private facilities designed for expatriates and foreign visitors
- **International/VIP departments of public hospitals** — separate wings with English-speaking staff, streamlined processes, and shorter wait times

## How Hospital Departments Look and What to Look For

When you approach a Chinese hospital, you see a large building complex. Look for these signs:

- **国际医疗部 (International Medical Department)** — a separate building or wing, often with its own entrance. The sign includes English text.
- **急诊 (Jízhěn / Emergency)** — red signage, usually a dedicated entrance with an ambulance bay. This is where you go for emergencies.
- **门诊 (Ménzhěn / Outpatient Clinic)** — the main building entrance where most patients enter. Massive crowds, long lines. Avoid this if you have an alternative.

International hospitals like United Family (和睦家) look different: smaller, cleaner signage, English first on all labels, and a receptionist who speaks to you in English the moment you walk in. The building exterior resembles a private clinic in your home country, not a massive public facility.

## Step-by-Step: Registration Through Pharmacy

Here is exactly what happens at each window in a Chinese public hospital. This sequence is the same at nearly every public hospital in the country.

### Step 1: Registration (挂号 / Guàhào)

You walk to the Registration Counter. This is usually a long counter with multiple windows, each labeled with a number. You see a crowd of people holding registration cards and queue tickets.

**What you do:**
1. Go to any open window.
2. Hand the staff member your passport.
3. Say the department you need. If you do not speak Chinese, show a translation on your phone. Common departments:
   - 内科 (Internal Medicine) — general illness, fever, stomach issues
   - 外科 (Surgery) — injuries, fractures, cuts
   - 皮肤科 (Dermatology) — rashes, skin problems
   - 骨科 (Orthopedics) — bone and joint problems
   - 耳鼻喉科 (ENT) — ear, nose, throat
   - 眼科 (Ophthalmology) — eye problems
4. Pay the registration fee (20-100 RMB depending on the doctor's seniority).
5. Receive a registration card with your queue number and the room number.

**At an international department:** You skip this entirely. A coordinator greets you at the reception desk, takes your passport, and handles registration on your behalf.

### Step 2: Wait for Your Turn

Find the department area. Screens above the doors show current queue numbers. When your number appears, enter the room. Wait times:
- Public hospital standard department: 1-3 hours
- Public hospital VIP wing: 15-30 minutes
- International hospital: 5-15 minutes

### Step 3: Doctor Consultation

The doctor sees you for 5-10 minutes in a standard public hospital. In a VIP or international setting, you get 15-30 minutes. The doctor writes prescriptions and test orders on a single slip of paper — keep this slip. You need it for every next step.

### Step 4: Payment (缴费 / Jiǎofèi)

Take the doctor's slip to the Payment Window. This is a separate counter, usually on the same floor. Multiple windows, long lines. Hand the slip and your payment method. The cashier:
- Scans your registration card
- Reads the doctor's orders from the slip
- Tells you the total amount
- Takes your payment and gives you a stamped receipt

**What insurance cards look like at the payment window:** At the international department, you hand the cashier your insurance card along with your passport. If the hospital has a direct billing agreement with your insurer, the cashier swipes your card and verifies coverage. You then sign a claim form and pay nothing upfront (or just the co-pay). If there is no direct billing, you pay the full amount and file a claim with your insurer afterward. Keep every receipt.

### Step 5: Tests or Imaging

If the doctor ordered blood work, X-rays, or other tests, the payment receipt directs you to the test center — often a different floor. Hand the receipt at the test registration desk. They give you a queue number and a collection tube (for blood) or direct you to a waiting area. Results are available anywhere from 30 minutes to the next day, depending on the test. You must collect results yourself (printed at a self-service machine that scans your registration card), then return to the doctor with the printout.

### Step 6: Pharmacy Pickup (药房 / Yàofáng)

If the doctor prescribed medicine, your payment receipt includes a pharmacy counter number. Go to the pharmacy — usually on the ground floor, a separate counter from payment. Hand your receipt to the pharmacist. They:
- Retrieve the prescribed medications
- Read the dosage instructions aloud in Chinese
- Hand you the medicine with a printed label showing your name, the drug name, and dosage

**At international hospitals:** The pharmacy is a single counter. The pharmacist speaks English. They explain dosage in detail.

At a public hospital pharmacy, the label is in Chinese. Verify the drug name against what the doctor told you. Use your phone to photograph the label and translate it before leaving the counter.

## Pharmacies and Over-the-Counter Medicine

Pharmacies (药店 / yàodiàn) are plentiful — look for the green cross sign. Over-the-counter medications for colds, headaches, allergies, and digestive issues are available without prescription. **Prescription medications require a Chinese doctor's prescription** — you cannot use a foreign prescription.

Pharmacists may not speak English. To get the right medication:
- Show them a photo of the medication packaging (if you know what you need)
- Write down the generic drug name (e.g., "ibuprofen," "loratadine")
- Use a translation app to describe your symptoms

## Emergency Medical Care

For emergencies, dial **120** for an ambulance. In a life-threatening situation, you can also go directly to the nearest hospital emergency room (急诊 / jízhěn).

Ambulance response times in major cities are typically 10-20 minutes. The ambulance will transport you to the nearest hospital with an emergency department — you do not choose the destination. Emergency room treatment is provided first; payment is handled afterward.

If you are conscious and the situation is not immediately life-threatening, and you have the option, going directly to an international hospital emergency department will result in a more familiar standard of care and communication.
